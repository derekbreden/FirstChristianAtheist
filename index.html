Acceptance

Christianity as it is today does not accept unrepentant atheists, with the exception of Unitarian Universalists whose services are targetted towards a theistic audience, focusing on the joys of worshipping God together.

Perhaps here we can have a sanctuary, for those few of us who have something we wish to salvage from the wreckage that is Christianity's encounter with the modern age, some value that we seek to carry forward without the theistic baggage.

My personal testimony

At age 6 I was given a choice to believe in Jesus or not. It was likely I had heard much about religion prior to this, but this is the first time I remember where I had some role of my own in the situation. I didn't understand what it would mean to believe, but I thought it was something that I could choose to do.

At age 9 I asked to be baptized, and I was.

At age 10 I was introduced to hypocrisy in the church, through the book "In His Steps" by Charles Sheldon, which asked the question "What would Jesus do?" Common church behavior did not live up to Sheldon's expectations, and I was able to see the "othering" happening in my own church, particularly towards unbelievers and what could be done to reach them, to help them repent of their unbelief.

At age 12 God spoke to me and said that he was an idea invented by man, changed over thousands of years.

I slowly felt alienated with other Christians, that the things I said and did were not understood by them. That what I understood Christianity to be about, unconditional love for all, was not what they understood Christianity to be about. For them it was about John 3:16. For me it was about Galations 5:14.

At age 23 I had a conversation with a former worship leader and friend, just a couple years older than me, that ended with him telling me that I was talking to the wrong person, that he was not the one I needed to talk to. I asked him who I needed to talk to? He said “I don’t know”. Earlier in our conversation, he told me that faith was important. I said I don’t know that. I asked him why is faith important? He said we grew up in the same church, how could I not know?

At age 28 I had another religious experience. It was clear to me that I was seeing signs in the real world that corresponded to my thoughts, as if the universe were reading my mind and all the events around me were arranged to affirm my beliefs. Much like the bird that flew in and out of the church at my grandmother's funeral that meant so much to my mother, her own mother's soul saying goodbye, I was seeing meaningful birds of this sort everywhere.

At no point did I ever see evidence of anything supernatural. I did have a repeat of my younger experiences, seeing and hearing God through my own imagination and my interpretation of the events around me, words of others I heard, words I found. I saw again how the most innocuous of coincidences with a little confirmation bias and motivated reasoning (where we ignore the tidal wave of events happening all the time, and instead focus on a few coincidences that can be seen as responses and confirmation of thoughts and prayers), I saw how this confirmation bias can be strong and convincing evidence that prayers are being answered always. I heard voices clearly, but they were also all very clearly generated by own mind, and in no way different from the ordinary inner monologue and imagination of what others might say, that we are all (almost all) familiar with.

The only difference was that during this episode the mundane took on special significance. I was able to see how (in a way that had been difficult for me to do for a long time) an adult could find themselves believing in the supernatural, even as I still failed to do so myself.

At age 38 I found Bishop John Shelby Spong. Finally, here was someone who thought Christianity was about what I thought it was about, unconditional love. John says it as “living life fully, loving wastefully, with no expectation of reward.” There is no need to say anything about sin, to ask anyone for mercy, to exclude anyone from being included in the Church.

And so I have some hope for the future of Christianity, to eventually be inclusive of those like myself who are not theists.
